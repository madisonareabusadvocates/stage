Starter Bus Rapid Transit Line

As the urban area in and around the City of Madison grows, our transit system must change to meet that growth. That change should include a regional transit system with a rapid transit that would keep downtown Madison attractive while keeping cars out of crowded streets. Transit-oriented development is not possible without the expectation of good, efficient transit. Bus rapid transit, or BRT, is a cost-effective initial mode of transit that could make the various parts of the region reachable for people travelling by foot or bicycle, and could thereby lead to better regional connections. Initial lines inside Madison could lead to the redevelopment of such places as Mineral Point Road and East Washington Avenue. We focus here on a possible starter line. Why Rapid Buses?

What people want most of all is good service, whether that is train or bus. Financially, rail starts making sense as the capacity of the system gets large since you can run multiple cars with only one driver, the most expensive part of the train. But because of the Madison Area's relatively small size and existing transit infrastructure, it makes more sense to start with buses. The critical element is the minimum level of service and initially, that is what will be reflected in timetables, not capacity requirements. For rapid transit, 10 to 15 minute service is really the minimum. Longer headways mean people need to plan their trips with schedules, something most choice riders are reluctant to do.

So, in the Madison Area, bus rapid transit gives us the most service for our money, and service is what attracts riders. With rapid transit, people will be able to quickly and conveniently get from one point on a corridor to another, something conventional buses cannot do.  Madison could start with something such as the red line mapped above, and gradually add other lines mapped below.  The fuller map connects naturally occurring corridors in Madison that can be extended out for a more regional scope. In time, after ridership grew to the point where bus rapid transit could not keep up with demand, we could upgrade to rail.  BRT is a lot like rail, but less expensive initially.  Buses could be electric with overhead wires (called trolleybuses), could benefit from signal priority like a train, and could have such features as automated ticket vending for convenience and speed.  The most speed is attained from having limited stops and operating in bus only lanes.

This is the normal process for upgrading transit in cities around the world.  Rapid Buses could come to Madison soon, and could be the catalyst for rail. Here are some examples.
