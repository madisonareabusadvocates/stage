Rapid Transit in the Madison Area

As the urban area in and around the City of Madison grows, our transit system must change to meet changed circumstances. It must include rapid transit that can keep downtown attractive and keep cars out of crowded streets. Transit-oriented development is not possible without the expectation of good, efficient transit. Bus rapid transit, or BRT, is a cost-effective initial mode of transit that could make the various parts of the region reachable for people travelling by foot, and could thereby lead to the redevelopment of such places in the City of Madison as Mineral Point Road and East Washington Avenue. We focus here on a starter line that could connect these two areas. A fuller schemata is shown at the end.

Why Rapid Buses?

What people want most of all is good service, whether that is train or bus. Financially, rail starts making sense as the capacity of the system gets large since you can run multiple cars with only one driver, the most expensive part of the train. But because of the Madison Area's relatively small size, it makes more sense to start with buses. That is, the critical element is the minimum level of service and initially, we won't be writing timetables based on capacity requirements. We'll be writing them to provide a reasonable amount of service.  For rapid transit, 10 to 15 minute service is really the minimum for a system to be truly rapid. Longer headways mean people need to plan their trips with schedules, something most choice riders are reluctant to do.

So, in Madison, bus rapid transit gives us the most service for our money, and service is what attracts riders.  The full map connects naturally occurring corridors in Madison that can be extended out for a more regional scope.  With rapid transit, people will be able to quickly and conveniently get from one point on a corridor to another, something conventional buses don't allow.  Madison could start with something such as the red line mapped above, and gradually add other lines.  In time, after ridership grew to the point where bus rapid transit couldn't keep up with demand, we could upgrade to rail.  BRT is a lot like rail, but without the expense of the infrastructure.  Buses could be electric with overhead wires (called trolleybuses), will benefit from signal priority like a train, and such features as automated ticket vending for convenience and speed.  The most speed is attained from having limited stops and operating in bus only lanes.

This is the normal process for upgrading transit in cities around the world.  Rapid Buses could come to Madison soon, and be the catalyst for Light Rail Transit.  Here are some examples.
