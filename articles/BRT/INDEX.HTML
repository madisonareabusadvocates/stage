Bus Rapid Transit for Madison

The City of Madison is growing, and as desirable things get placed farther out we will need to be able to reach them with transit.  This is a proposal for implementing the Madison Metro Mover, nicknamed The M.  This idea is only in its infancy, an idea will likely change and evolve as it moves forward.  Rapid transit is the way to serve a growing city, and it also benefits the downtown by making transit more attractive and keeping cars out of crowded streets.  Transit oriented development is not possible without good efficient transit.  Bus rapid transit, or BRT, could make other parts of the city reachable for the crowd that travels on foot, and so, can lead to redevelopment of places like Mineral Point Road and East Washington Avenue.  Periphery routes that previously all had to come downtown to attract commuters can serve as local circulators, making their rides more efficient and opening up more cross-town trips to potential bus riders.

Vehicles - Could be a standard city transit bus, articulated bus, electric trolleybus, CNG powered bus, hybrid electric bus, or any combination.  Doors would likely open automatically and boarding would be pre-paid.

Service - Would likely operate at 10-minute intervals at rush hour, 15-minutes on weekends and non-peak hours.

Detail of Capitol Square - This proposal includes expanding Madison's downtown car-free network to include the capitol square.  The white lane dividers are painted yellow, indicating 2-way traffic.  Only bus lines, bicycles, and special vehicles like delivery trucks and state employees (who park on the square) are allowed, similar to State Street.  Traffic signals are removed to improve the flow of buses.

Rapid Transit Features - Include signal preemption, limited stops, transit only lanes along much of the line, and ticket vending and level boarding at stations.  Separate guided transit ways are not included.  When we're ready to make that kind of investment, we'll upgrade to light rail.

Capital Cost - 12 buses, 2 park and rides, 40 major stations, 16 minor stations, pavement marking, signal priority, vending machines and card readers, maintenance facility expansion.  Milwaukee has a similar proposal called the COMET that is $55 million for two lines.

Why bus rapid transit?

There has been a lot of talk about different forms of transit for Madison, especially rail.  The argument is, basically, that people like trains, and if you want to get people on transit, give them trains.  Arguably, that's not 100% right.  What people like is good service.  They equate good service to rail because just about any rail transit system is going to have good service.  The reason is that rail is expensive, so if you're a big city like Chicago or New York, you can afford to build the rail system and stock it heavily with trains.  So, specific technology does not attract the most users, the service does.  Financially, rail starts making sense as the capacity of the system gets large since you can run multiple cars with only one driver, the most expensive part of the vehicle.

Because of Madison's relatively small size, the critical element is the minimum level of service.  That is, we won't be writing timetables based on the capacity requirements, we'll be writing them to provide a reasonable amount of service.  For rapid transit, 10 to 15 minute service is really the minimum for a system to be truly rapid transit.  Longer headways mean people need to plan their trips with schedules, which most choice riders are reluctant to do, and waiting that long for a vehicle makes the trip less than "rapid".  At 10 minute service, we don't need the high capacity of light rail yet, and that's why the studies have shown it to be cost prohibitive.

So, in Madison, bus rapid transit gives us the most service for our money, and service is what attracts riders.  These maps were drawn connecting the naturally occurring corridors in Madison with the idea that they may connect to Verona, Sun Prairie, Middleton, and other towns with limited service as the lines are extended.  With rapid transit, people will realize that they can quickly and conveniently get from one point on a corridor to another, something conventional buses don't allow.  Madison could start a system like this with the red line, and gradually add other lines that connect.  In time, ridership will grow, and when bus rapid transit can't keep up with demand, we can upgrade to light rail.  The BRT line(s) will help bring up that demand when people find they can expect convenient travel on transit in Madison.  Buses could be electric with overhead wires (called trolleybuses), will benefit from signal priority like a train, and could feature things like automated ticket vending for convenience and speed.  The most speed is attained from having limited stops like light rail and operating in bus only lanes over most or all of the routes.

This is getting to be a fairly "normal" process for upgrading transit in cities around the world, and many cities in the United States.  Bus rapid transit can come to Madison soon, we are eligible for federal funding and there are fewer hurdles than with LRT.  BRT can also work very well with the proposed Streetcar and Transport 2020 Commuter Rail proposals.  Here are some exciting examples of new bus rapid transit lines.
